CHAPTER 1: The Dream of a Cow
───────────────────────────────────────────

Hori Ram had dreamed of owning a cow for as long as he could remember. This dream was not the vague, general aspiration that men attach to things they do not expect to obtain — the lottery ticket mentality, the idle fancy. It was specific, practical, and had been the object of real calculation: how much a cow would cost, what she would produce, how the extra income from the milk would allow the family to navigate the months when the harvest was uncertain.

A cow was also, for Hori, something beyond economics. In the village of Semari, as in villages throughout this part of the Gangetic plain, a man without a cow was a man of incomplete respectability. The cow-owning household had a standing that translated into a dozen practical advantages — the respect of neighbors, the attention of the moneylender, the confidence that comes from not being at the absolute bottom of the order. Hori was not at the absolute bottom. He owned his land — a very small amount of it, worked to exhaustion, mortgaged against debts that did not seem to diminish despite the annual payment — but he did not own a cow, and this absence announced itself in the social arithmetic of the village with a subtlety that he felt constantly.

He was perhaps forty years of age when this story finds him, though he looked older. Agricultural labor in the fields of Awadh, in the heat of summer and the damp of monsoon, under the pressure of debt and the weight of family obligation, does not preserve a man. He was thin in the way of men who work harder than they eat, with calloused hands and a back that had begun, in recent years, to register its objections to the bending.

His wife Dhania was the opposite: still round-faced and vigorous, with the energy of a woman who has decided that the situation does not call for giving up, whatever the situation happens to be.

CHAPTER 2: Hori's Village, Hori's World
───────────────────────────────────────────

The village of Semari was not poor in the way that places without resources are poor. The land was good land — the Ganges alluvial plain, faithful to the farmer who could work it. There was water. There were markets within reasonable distance. The problem was not the absence of resources but the distribution of them — the way in which the existing arrangements ensured that the value of the land flowed predominantly toward those who already had more than they needed.

Zamindars like Rai Sahib held title to the land that men like Hori worked. The system was old enough to have acquired the appearance of the natural order. Hori did not, in his daily thought, frame his situation as injustice. He framed it as circumstance — the way things were, the conditions within which he must operate as skillfully as possible. This was not stupidity. It was the practical adaptation of a man who understands that the energy spent in lamenting the rules of the game is energy that cannot be spent playing it.

His brothers Hira and Shobha were part of the same household in the way of joint family arrangements — legally, at least, though the emotional reality was more complex. The three brothers farmed the same land, shared the same courtyard, and conducted a running negotiation about resources, responsibilities, and the various accumulated grievances that any long-term proximity between people produces. Hori was the eldest and the mediating figure. He was patient with his brothers in the way that he was patient with his land — accepting their difficult seasons without abandoning the relationship.

The village moneylender, Datadin, was the other inescapable figure in Hori's life — the creditor before whom the family's debt had accumulated across years of short harvests and emergency borrowings and interest that compounded with reliable efficiency. Datadin was not a monster. He was a man operating within the same system as everyone else, and the system had arranged itself to produce the result that Hori found himself in, regardless of anyone's individual character.

───────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER 3: The Landlord's Shadow
───────────────────────────────────────────

Rai Sahib maintained his distance from the daily life of Semari in the manner of landlords who understand that presence makes them available for requests they prefer not to grant. He appeared at the time of the harvest — to ensure his portion — and at the time of festivals — to receive the ceremonies of respect that reaffirmed the hierarchical order — and at other times only when something required his personal adjudication.

Hori's dealings with Rai Sahib were conducted through intermediaries and through the rituals of deference that both parties understood and neither chose to examine. When Hori had to approach the landowner directly — to request a deferral of rent during a bad year, or to explain why the full payment was not possible — he went with the particular combination of dignity and submission that the situation required: upright enough to preserve his self-respect, deferential enough to have a chance of success.

Rai Sahib was not indifferent to the condition of his tenants in the abstract. He had ideas about social responsibility, about the duty of the propertied toward those who worked the land. These ideas were genuinely held, and they informed his philanthropic activities in the town — donations to schools, participation in public ceremonies, the maintenance of a reputation for civic-mindedness.

But the individual encounter between Rai Sahib's principles and Hori's actual situation tended to resolve in the way that such encounters always tend to resolve: the principle survived and the situation did not improve, because improving it would have required a structural change that Rai Sahib's principles were not equipped to demand of himself.

Hori understood this. He did not condemn Rai Sahib for it. He understood that men are creatures of their positions as well as of their characters, and that the position of a landlord in this system made certain behaviors not just possible but nearly automatic.

He bowed the appropriate degree and made his case as well as he could and walked home with whatever he had managed to obtain.

CHAPTER 4: Dhania's Strength
───────────────────────────────────────────

Dhania had opinions about everything and kept very few of them to herself. This was not, as some of Hori's acquaintances suggested, a defect of character. It was, rather, the quality of a woman who had decided that the alternative — the patient, silent absorption of everything — was not available to her without cost that she was not willing to pay.

She loved Hori with the practical love of long partnership — the love that manifests less as feeling and more as the constant activity of managing a shared life. She worried about him with the specific worry of a woman who can see what her husband is not able to see because he is too close to it: the toll the debt was taking, the way each year left him slightly less than the year before, the direction in which all of this was trending.

She was harder than he was in her assessments, more impatient with the social performances that were costing them something they could not afford. When Hori proposed the purchase of a cow — a purchase he had finally scraped together the approximate funds for, through a combination of small savings and a loan he had arranged — she was both enthusiastic and suspicious: enthusiastic because she understood exactly what a cow would mean, suspicious because the numbers Hori described did not add up to safety.

"The loan," she said. "What is the interest?"

Hori told her.

"And we can pay this from the milk money."

"Yes. If the cow is healthy."

Dhania looked at him. "If."

The cow was purchased. It was a fine animal by the standards available to them — young, with good markings. The neighborhood came to see it. For a few days, the courtyard of Hori's house had a quality of festival. Dhania milked the cow in the mornings with the satisfaction of a woman whose practical calculation has been confirmed by reality.

The disaster, when it came, came quickly.

───────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER 5: The City and the Village
───────────────────────────────────────────

Gobind, Hori's son, went to the city. This was not unusual — the cities were drawing the young men of the villages with the urgency of a current that once entered is difficult to resist. What the city offered was the possibility of cash wages, of a life not subject to the annual terror of the monsoon's adequacy, of a modernity that the village could not provide.

Gobind found work in a mill and sent money home when he could, which was less regularly than he intended and more than many managed. He wrote letters in the careful hand of someone who learned to write as an adult and treats each letter as an achievement. The letters were read aloud by the schoolteacher's son, who served as the village's general literary resource, and Dhania listened with her face arranged in the expression that mothers wear when they are not sure whether good news or bad news is more likely.

The city, in Premchand's telling, was not simply liberation. It was also dislocation — the extraction of people from the communities that had sustained them into a new order that had not yet developed equivalent supports. Gobind was earning; Gobind was also alone in a way that the village had never allowed him to be alone, surrounded by strangers whose indifference was structural rather than personal.

Meanwhile, back in Semari, the pressures on the land continued their patient accumulation. The cow — the cow that had been the dream — had not survived the first year. A disease had taken her with the randomness that diseases in animals and in the lives of the poor operate: without malice, without mercy, and without any concern for the meaning that the animal had carried.

Hori paid for the loss with his back and with the years that the back's labor cost him.

───────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER 6: The Gift
───────────────────────────────────────────

In the final passage of his life, Hori was working on a road construction project — hired labor, daily wages, the kind of work that a man takes when his other options have been exhausted. He was not well. Those who knew him could see this. Dhania could see it with the clarity of a woman who has watched someone so closely for so long that their deterioration is legible to her before it is legible to them.

He continued working because stopping was not a choice that his situation permitted. The debt had not resolved; it had simply changed its shape and its creditors. The land was still there, worked now by the arrangement of family members who remained, but the family's claim on its own output was as constrained as it had ever been.

The day that Hori died, he had earned a small amount — a few annas — in wages. This is how Premchand tells it: the dying man's final concern is not transcendence or legacy or the resolution of emotional complexity. It is the question of whether there is enough for the godan — the ritual gift of a cow that a dying Hindu makes, so that the soul may cross the river of death.

There was not enough for a cow. There had never been enough for a cow. The dream of the cow had animated his entire working life and had not been realized, and the dream itself had become a kind of mockery of the gap between aspiration and possibility in the life of the Indian peasant.

Dhania, in the final moment, gave the priest the coins that remained and asked him to accept, symbolically, the offering that could not be made in fact. The godan — the gift of a cow that was not there.

The priest accepted the coins. The ritual was performed with the materials available.

This is the end of the story, and it is unbearable, and it is exact, and it is why the book has not been forgotten.
