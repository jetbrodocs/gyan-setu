TALE 1: The Oath of Tejsi Thakor
───────────────────────────────────────────

In the kingdom of Kathiawar, in the time before the railways had crossed the Bhadar river, there lived a chieftain named Tejsi Thakor whose name, by the time this story begins, had become larger than the man himself — which is the peculiar fate of those who have performed a single act of absolute courage in a world that hungers for such acts and remembers them long after the person has become dust and the circumstances have been forgotten.

The act that made Tejsi's name was this: he had, in the presence of the assembled chiefs of the region, given his word that a guest under his roof would not be harmed. The guest was a man his enemies wanted. His enemies were numerous and powerful. His own forces were not.

He sent word to his enemies: "Come and take him if you can. But know that you will enter my house only over my body, and you will leave it only if I permit, and if either of these things proves untrue, then everything I have ever called myself is a lie."

His enemies weighed the man against the principle and decided, after some deliberation, that the principle was the kind of thing that, if broken visibly, creates more trouble than it resolves. They withdrew.

The guest was escorted safely to the border.

Tejsi was left with nothing but his reputation, which is to say he was left with everything.

The people of Saurashtra told this story for generations, and in the telling, certain details shifted and simplified and the colors grew more vivid, as stories always do when they become the property of the community rather than of the individual. But the central fact remained: a man had given his word and kept it at great cost, and this was considered the most remarkable and admirable thing a person could do.

That the guest was not especially worthy of the sacrifice, that he was a minor official with uncertain loyalties and questionable motives, was not considered relevant. The point was not the guest. The point was the word.

TALE 2: The Merchant's Daughter and the Salt Road
───────────────────────────────────────────

The salt road from the Rann to the markets of the interior had been traveled for a thousand years, and every family along its length had its stories of the road — the storms that came without warning, the bandits who were sometimes generous, the wells that were there in one season and gone in the next, the inns where the beds were made of rope and the food was made of necessity.

Mamta was the daughter of a salt merchant who had died when she was twelve, leaving a debt, a cart, a mule named Budho, and a business that no one in the family had any experience running. Her older brother was in Bhavnagar, engaged in some unspecified enterprise that never produced visible results. Her mother had the resourcefulness of a woman who had always had someone else to be resourceful for her.

So Mamta learned the road.

She was not supposed to travel it alone. Custom was quite clear on this matter, as custom tended to be on most matters involving women in motion. But the debt was real, the mule was available, and Mamta had made a calculation: the danger of the road was less certain than the danger of the debt.

She traveled with a thick shawl pulled over her head in the manner of an older woman, and she carried a stick that she did not know how to use but that looked as though she might. She spoke to no one she did not need to speak to, and when she needed to speak she spoke in a direct, unornamented way that discouraged the kind of conversation that was not about business.

In three years, she had paid the debt, replaced the mule (Budho having died peacefully in his sleep in the second year, mourned by Mamta more than she would have predicted), and established a reputation on the salt road as someone who was absolutely reliable with accounts and absolutely uninteresting to bother.

This reputation was the one she had worked hardest to build, and she wore it as some women wear jewelry — not for others, but because it pleased her to know it was there.

TALE 3: Ratno Ratnavali
───────────────────────────────────────────

They said in the villages around Gondal that Ratno the potter could speak to clay — that the material changed in his hands not just because of skill but because of some communication that passed between the man and the earth he worked.

This was the kind of thing that gets said about craftsmen who are very good at what they do, and it is always partly false and partly true. It is false in the literal sense; clay does not hear. It is true in the sense that the best craftsmen develop a relationship with their materials that resembles a dialogue — a constant questioning and response, a sensitivity to what the material wants to be that shapes the craftsman's hands as much as the hands shape the material.

Ratno had been making pots since he was eight, sitting beside his father in the courtyard of their house in Gondal, learning by watching and then by doing and then by making the ten thousand mistakes that eventually produce competence. By the time he was thirty, his pots were known in markets as far as Rajkot — not for their decoration, which was restrained, but for their soundness. A pot by Ratno did not crack. A pot by Ratno held water for longer. A pot by Ratno survived the journey from market to kitchen and the years of daily use.

He was not wealthy, which was the natural condition of craftsmen who made things well but did not think about commerce. He sold what he made for what seemed fair and spent what he needed and gave away the rest to whomever needed it, which was always more people than he had things to give.

His wife managed the money with the quiet efficiency of a woman who has accepted that her husband is constitutionally unable to accumulate and has decided to be the accumulator for both of them. They were comfortable. They had two children. They had the respect of their neighbors, which in Gondal was the currency that mattered most.

And Ratno had his clay, which every morning presented itself to him as it always had — patient, potential, waiting to become what his hands and his particular understanding of the world would make it.

TALE 4: The Last Camel of Halvad
───────────────────────────────────────────

The town of Halvad had once been a place where camel trains stopped regularly — the great slow convoys from Rajputana and Sindh that brought spices, dyes, and cloth and took back cotton and salt and ghee. The camel drivers were a community unto themselves, with their own songs and their own slang and their own relationships with particular inns and particular wells along the routes they traveled.

But the railway had come, and then the truck, and the camel trains had thinned to a trickle and then to nothing. The last camel in Halvad belonged to an old Rabari herdsman named Vago, who had owned it for fifteen years and had no intention of selling it and could not, he explained to anyone who asked, imagine his life without it.

The camel's name was Mohan, which was a name more commonly given to children, but Vago had named it when the camel was young and had seemed, in some quality of its expression, to merit a dignified name. Mohan had the serene and slightly contemptuous expression that camels wear as a matter of course, and which in Mohan's case Vago had always interpreted as a form of wisdom rather than disdain.

They were, by the time the story reaches them, both old. Vago moved slowly and rested more than he used to. Mohan's knees gave him trouble on cold mornings and he rose with a complaint that was clearly theatrical but also, Vago thought, not entirely unjustified.

The new generation of Halvad found them both an amusing anachronism. They took photographs of the old man and his camel with the cameras that everyone now carried in their pockets, which Vago bore with the patience of one who has long ago decided that the opinions of the young are a weather system — noisy, temporary, and best waited out.

Mohan had no opinion on the matter. He ate what was available and endured what was asked of him and regarded the world with the steady, hooded indifference that was his permanent expression, and that Vago, who knew him well, could distinguish precisely — the slight tension around the eye that meant hunger, the particular angle of the neck that meant contentment, the small twitch of the lower lip that meant he had noticed something interesting.

They were the last of something. They knew this. They did not, as far as Vago could tell, find this particularly troubling.

TALE 5: The Shepherd's Riddle
───────────────────────────────────────────

A shepherd who tends sheep on the hills above Chotila has a lot of time to think, and this either produces wisdom or nonsense, depending on the quality of the shepherd. Bhura had been tending sheep on the hills above Chotila for forty years and had produced, in that time, both wisdom and nonsense in proportions that his neighbors found characteristic.

He was known for asking questions that sounded simple and were not. He would ask them at the well, or at the threshing, or at the tea stall near the market road, and then he would wait, with the patience of a man who has spent forty years in the company of animals that move at their own pace, for whatever answer came.

His most famous question, which was discussed for years in Chotila and the surrounding villages, was this: "If a man is faithful all his life to something that turns out to be false, has he wasted his life or spent it well?"

People argued about this question in the way they argued about cricket scores and rainfall — with heat but without any real expectation of resolution. The pious said that faithfulness to truth was its own reward and therefore the question contained a flaw. The practical said that results matter and a life built on falsehood is a life built on sand. The poets said the beauty of the faithfulness was independent of the object of that faithfulness.

Bhura listened to all these answers and nodded at each one in the way that suggested he found them all interesting but none of them quite right.

When pressed for his own answer, he said: "The sheep knows its shepherd even when the shepherd is lost. That is the answer."

No one quite understood this, which Bhura considered satisfactory. A riddle whose answer is immediately understood has not done its work.

He returned to the hills. The sheep followed.
