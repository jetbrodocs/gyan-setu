          MEGHADUTA
          (The Cloud Messenger)
          by Kalidasa
          ~4th–5th century CE (Gupta period)
═══════════════════════════════════════════

[Note: English translation/prose rendering for demo purposes.
Originally composed in Sanskrit in the Mandakranta metre.
Kalidasa is considered the greatest poet in the Sanskrit literary tradition.
This is a kavya (lyric poem) of 111 verses in two sections: Purvamegha
(Prior Cloud) and Uttaramegha (Posterior Cloud).]

───────────────────────────────────────────
INTRODUCTION: The Yaksha's Exile
───────────────────────────────────────────

On the sacred mountain of Ramagiri, in the hermitages where the trees give plentiful shade and whose pools have been made holy by the bathing of Sita, a Yaksha lived out his days of exile. He was a servant of the Lord of Wealth, Kubera, who rules the northern mountains and all that is precious within them, and he had been cast from his place of service through some unnamed negligence of duty — the great lord's anger swift, the punishment certain and complete: one year in exile, far from his home, far from his beloved.

Months had passed. The rainy season had come. And the Yaksha, his body thin from the absence of the pleasures that sustain Yakshas, his mind habituated to the dull ache of separation that becomes, over time, a kind of chronic condition rather than an acute pain, sat on the peak of the mountain and watched the monsoon cloud climb the slope of the hill like a dark and purposeful traveler.

The sight of the cloud broke something open in him. In the ordinary logic of things, a cloud is not a messenger. It cannot carry words, cannot convey specific meanings, cannot ensure that what is sent arrives intact at its destination. The Yaksha knew this. He was not, the poet tells us, ignorant of the distinction between living things and those without minds.

But the yearning of those who are separated from what they love operates outside the ordinary logic of things. It is the nature of longing to invest the world with the capacity to carry meaning — to see in the flight of a bird the answer to an unasked question, to find in the pattern of clouds some correspondence to the pattern of one's thought.

And so the Yaksha addressed the cloud.

───────────────────────────────────────────
VERSES 1–15: The Cloud Encountered
───────────────────────────────────────────

"O Cloud, who are the friend of those who are separated from their loved ones, I ask you to carry my message to my beloved who lives in Alaka, the city of Yakshas in the northern mountains, near Kailasha where the lord Shiva dwells.

"You who are formed of water and light and wind and darkness, who carry the rains that make the earth green, who are greeted by the peacocks with their dancing and by the thirsty fields with their rising fragrance — you who are worthy of carrying even the most sacred of messages — hear me.

"I know that you are without thought and I am a being who thinks, and that the union of a thinking being with one who lacks thought is the kind of unequal alliance that wise men warn against. But the heart in its need does not take counsel from wisdom. And you are here, and you are moving northward, in the direction of everything I love.

"Your body is a dark beauty — the color of the fresh blue lotus, the color of the sapphire that Kubera's treasury contains. Your belly is heavy with water; when you rest on a mountain peak, you look like an elephant kneeling on a dark hill. Your lightning is your playmate, restless and bright, never still for long. Your thunder is the sound of the kettledrum at the marriage of the rain-gods.

"Go northward. Take the route I will describe to you, past the cities and the mountains and the sacred rivers that lie between this hill and the city where she waits. And when you arrive, find the house I will describe — the house with the flowers in the courtyard, the house with the painting on the threshold — and give her my message."

───────────────────────────────────────────
VERSES 16–35: The Cloud's Journey Northward
───────────────────────────────────────────

The Yaksha described the route with the precision of someone who has made the journey many times in memory and knows each landmark with the intimacy of longing.

"First you will pass over Ujjain, the city beloved of Shiva, whose women are beautiful and whose streets are full of the scent of flowers from their hair. Do not pass Ujjain without paying your respects: let your rain fall on the temple rooftops, let your thunder wake the devotees to their prayers, let the lightning illuminate the golden spires before you move on.

"You will cross the Vetravati river, which flows with the joy of one who has just been united with her beloved. You will see the Vindhya ranges, dark and wooded, where the ascetics sit in meditation under trees that have grown around them over decades. Honor them as you pass — let your shadow give them cool respite from the heat before the rains.

"You will cross the great plain of the north, where the farmers will already be looking at you with the anxious love of those whose survival depends on whether you are generous or withhold. Be generous. This is not your destination, but generosity requires no destination.

"You will reach the Himalaya at last — the great white range that separates the world of men from the world of the divine, where the rivers of India are born, where Ganga descends from heaven to earth. Here the air itself changes quality: it is purer, thinner, carrying the fragrance of Himalayan flowers and the silence of great altitude.

"Climb the mountains. Cross the passes where the wind is fierce and cold and the snow does not melt even in summer. Below you will see the lakes that are turquoise from the sky's reflection, the glaciers that move in imperceptible slowness toward the valleys. And beyond the highest passes, in the protected valley where the air is always cool and the flowers are always the flowers of paradise — there is Alaka."

───────────────────────────────────────────
VERSES 36–65: The City of Alaka and the Beloved
───────────────────────────────────────────

"Alaka is a city such as exists nowhere else in the three worlds. Its gardens are tended by the wish-granting trees, which produce whatever flower or fruit one desires. Its streets are paved with precious stones. Its houses rise to such height that their upper terraces catch the clouds as they pass — the women of Alaka sometimes gather water from the clouds directly, without descending to the rivers below.

"The Mandakini river flows through the city, filled with the lotus flowers that the Yaksha women wear. In the evenings, the light on the river is golden-red, and the women bathe there laughing, and the sound of their laughter echoes from the crystalline walls of the city.

"My house you will recognize by the mango tree in the courtyard, which is old and large enough that its branches spread over three-quarters of the space. At its base I planted a priyangu vine in the first year of our marriage, and it has grown around the mango's trunk now in the way of things that have committed themselves entirely to what they love.

"She will be in the house. She has not left the house much, since my exile — I know this because I know her, and I know that her grief requires enclosure, that she processes sorrow the way the earth processes rain, inward and slow and thorough.

"She will be thin. The garlands she used to wear every day will have been abandoned; she does not dress for herself alone. She will have lost some of the brightness she had when I was there, not because the brightness was dependent on me, but because brightness requires companionship — requires an audience of one particular person who receives it and reflects it back.

"When you find her, identify yourself to her — let your shadow fall across her threshold so that she knows something has come from outside, something large and purposeful. And then give her my words."

───────────────────────────────────────────
VERSES 66–111: The Message
───────────────────────────────────────────

"Tell her that I am alive. This is the first and most important thing. Tell her that the exile continues but that I remain, and that remaining is what I do in the service of our reunion.

"Tell her that the hermitage on Ramagiri is pleasant in its way — the trees provide shade, the pools are clear, the holy character of the place creates an atmosphere of calm that is not unwelcome to one who needs calm. But tell her that calm without her is only the absence of turbulence, not the presence of peace. Peace is what happens when she is in the same room.

"Tell her I still count the days. Tell her I have not lost count.

"Tell her that the memories I have of her are the food I eat — not literally, not in the way of bread, but in the way of what sustains the spirit. I think of the way she laughed. I think of the way she arranged flowers in the morning, never to a formal pattern but to some intuition of rightness that I always found, when I looked at the result, to be exactly right. I think of her hands.

"Tell her that this separation has not diminished her in my mind. On the contrary: distance has clarified her, in the way that things seen from far away reveal their essential shape. I understand, at this distance, what she is to me in a way that proximity, with its thousand small adjustments, does not always permit.

"Tell her to endure. Not passively — I know she is not passive — but in the particular way of one who endures with purpose, who keeps the inner life active and warm against the day when the outer life resumes its full dimension.

"Tell her that the year is not yet done but it will be done. That the cloud carries my love to her not because clouds can carry love — I know they cannot — but because I have placed it there with enough force that perhaps something of it will arrive, if not in words then in the quality of the rain that falls, warm from my direction, on the house where she waits."

───────────────────────────────────────────
CODA: The Cloud Departs
───────────────────────────────────────────

The Yaksha fell silent. The cloud, which had gathered and darkened during the speech, now moved. It did not speak — clouds do not speak. But it moved northward, as clouds do, following the pattern of wind and moisture that carries the monsoon toward the mountains, unhurried, purposeful in its own meteorological way.

The Yaksha watched it until it was gone over the curve of the hill.

Kalidasa ends the poem here, without telling us whether the message arrives, whether the beloved receives it, whether the cloud paused over Alaka long enough for its shadow to fall meaningfully across a threshold. The separation continues beyond the poem's ending.

This is, in the tradition of Sanskrit kavya, the correct ending. The poem is not about resolution. It is about the state of longing itself — its richness, its specificity, the way it generates, from the absence of what is loved, an extraordinary vividness of what is loved. The absent wife, described in such detail, becomes more present than many a present person. The route to Alaka, described with such geographical and emotional precision, becomes a landscape of the mind that readers have walked in imagination for sixteen centuries.

The cloud is not a messenger. The cloud is a poem.
