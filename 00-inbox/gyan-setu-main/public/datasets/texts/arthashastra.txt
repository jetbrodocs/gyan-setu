BOOK I, CHAPTER 1: The Life of the King — Training in Vedic Learning
───────────────────────────────────────────

This treatise has been composed by Kautilya for the acquisition and protection of this world and the next.

Of all the sciences, the science of politics is the most excellent, since it deals with the fruit of those sciences — a prosperous state — and guards them. It is the foundation of all other sciences. It is called Arthashastra because it treats of the means of livelihood and profit (artha) of men, and also because it treats of the Earth (prithvi).

The king who is possessed of self-control and who cultivates intellect by association with elders, ever and always perceives his welfare through the science of polity. He should remain ever active and spend his energy in acquiring knowledge; for knowledge has no satiety for one who thirsts after it.

He should devote himself to the study of Vedic lore, the three branches of philosophy (Anvikshiki, Vedic study, and economics), as well as to the science of politics. He should learn the exercise of his limbs from those who are proficient in the use of weapons and in horsemanship. He should hear the science of politics from those who are well-versed in the management of state affairs, and devote himself to the practice of what he has learned from them.

He should keep his time distributed as follows: he should devote half a day to learning, and a sixth part to governing; the remainder of the day may be given to the conduct of his personal affairs. But if he is not capable of devoting his whole time to state affairs, he should associate with him ministers and councilors for the purpose.

A king who is well-educated and disciplined in sciences, devoted to good government of his subjects, intent on their welfare and the uplift of the people, and possessed of high character, can alone be the ruler and protector.

BOOK I, CHAPTER 7: The Duties of Government Superintendents
───────────────────────────────────────────

The king shall appoint as collectors of revenue such persons as are born of noble families, and who are honest, enthusiastic, capable, and possessed of foresight. Those who are not susceptible to bribery and who can successfully collect revenue from people in different circumstances, shall be appointed as revenue collectors.

The superintendent of mines shall examine ore-bearing spots both on plains and mountains. The superintendent of ocean trade shall know the ways of the seas and shall govern trade on the ocean. The superintendent of forests shall look after all the affairs of forests and the animals and timber that are in them.

The accountant general shall have all the officers of government and their transactions under his control. He shall have a complete knowledge of all receipts and disbursements, and of all officers and their performance of their duties.

The chamberlain shall keep a detailed account of the king's property — the gems, gold and silver, cloth, carriages, elephants, horses, cattle, storehouses, and all that is deposited in the treasury. He shall keep a day-to-day record of the income and expenditure.

Every superintendent shall carry out his own departmental work, avoid false dealings, spend money only on authorised objects, increase the revenue and decrease the expenditure. Every officer shall be personally responsible to the king for any money that is lost through his neglect.

A man is honest if he does not misappropriate public money for his own use. He is a good man if he is skilled and talented and does his work efficiently. He is a good government servant who keeps the king's interests at heart and administers according to the rules.

BOOK II, CHAPTER 1: On the Formation of Villages
───────────────────────────────────────────

The king shall people the countryside, either by shifting some of the inhabitants of his own country, or by inviting emigrants from foreign lands. He shall construct in the countryside settlements of various sizes — those that contain from one hundred to five hundred families of agriculturalists of the Shudra caste, chiefly, shall be formed into villages. The villages shall be separated from one another by a distance of two miles or more.

Each village shall have its boundaries clearly marked by rivers, mountains, forests, stone landmarks, and the like. The villages shall be arranged in groups of ten, twenty, hundred, and thousand. No village shall become too large or too small.

The territory of the state shall be so administered that no district is left without its designated village, no village without its boundaries marked, and no farm without its owner. The administrator shall be responsible for ensuring that every piece of land is properly cultivated.

He shall provide for the villages with seed and food in times of distress, and remit taxes and lend money free of interest to the industrious poor. He shall construct reservoirs, tanks, canals, roads, and such public works as the people need. He shall protect the villagers from oppression by powerful persons, from robbers, and from animals that harm their crops.

The king who takes his share of what the earth, the cattle, and the people produce shall care for them as a father cares for his children. If the king exercises oppression, the kingdom will be impoverished; the subjects will migrate; the wells and irrigation channels will go to ruin; and neither crops, pasture, forests, nor mines will thrive.

BOOK III, CHAPTER 1: Concerning Law
───────────────────────────────────────────

Law (dharma) is based upon truth. Judicial evidence is based on witnesses and documents. Custom and royal decree form the basis of jurisprudence. In the absence of specific law, the king shall decide cases by equity and custom.

The function of law is the protection of people. That which is beneficial to all is regarded as good law; that which is not beneficial to all, or that which protects only the powerful, is not good law. A king who makes and enforces just laws gives prosperity to all.

There shall be three kinds of judicial courts: the principal court, the secondary court, and the court of the chief justice. The principal court shall decide cases arising among the citizens. The secondary court shall adjudicate cases not resolved by the principal court. The chief justice shall hear appeals and ensure that the courts below have acted in accordance with the rules of evidence.

Judges shall be selected from among those who are learned, impartial, truthful, and of good conduct. They shall not accept gifts from those whose cases are before them. They shall not favor their own relatives, and they shall not decide cases quickly without proper investigation.

In all civil disputes, the burden of proof rests with the one who makes the claim. No man shall be punished before the charge against him has been fully heard and investigated. Evidence that has been given under compulsion shall not be accepted. A confession made under fear shall not be relied upon.

The king himself shall be the ultimate court of appeal. He shall sit in court at least one day in three and shall give ear to the petitions of his subjects. He who closes his ears to the petitions of his subjects shall soon find that he has no subjects.

BOOK VII, CHAPTER 1: The Six-Fold Policy
───────────────────────────────────────────

The six forms of state policy (shadgunya) which are applicable in the conduct of foreign relations are: peace (sandhi), war (vigraha), marching (yana), halting or waiting (asana), allying with one power against another (dvaidhibhava), and seeking the protection of a stronger power (samshraya).

A king who is weaker than his enemy shall make peace. A king who is stronger than his enemy may wage war. A king who desires to achieve something that the existing situation does not permit shall march and make preparation. A king who is neither stronger nor weaker shall remain still and consolidate his position. A king who seeks to avoid being consumed by two enemies on either side shall seek an alliance with one against the other. A king who faces destruction from all sides shall seek the protection of a powerful overlord.

The six policies are not applied singly but in combination according to circumstance. The wise king knows which combination serves his purpose at each moment, and does not allow himself to be bound by a policy that the changing situation has made inappropriate.

The test of policy is result. A policy that achieves its object is good policy. A policy that fails to achieve its object, however theoretically correct, is not good policy. The king who is rigid in his adherence to a method and neglects the outcome has inverted the proper order of things.

The king who wishes to extend his power must first be certain of his own base: his treasury, his army, his administration, and the loyalty of his people. Without a secure base, expansion is not power but exposure. Without a prosperous treasury, even the most just king cannot maintain his administration. Without a capable and loyal army, the king cannot protect what he governs. Without good administration, neither treasury nor army can function over time.

Therefore the first duty of the king is to himself — not in the sense of self-interest, but in the sense of self-preparation. A lamp that has not enough oil cannot give light to others.
