          THE DISCOVERY OF INDIA
          by Jawaharlal Nehru

CHAPTER 1: At Ahmednagar Fort
───────────────────────────────────────────

Five months of a long imprisonment had passed, and I sat again at my desk in the barrack room, watching the sun go down behind the great battlements of the fort. The shadows lengthened across the courtyard, the light turned from gold to amber to the deep rose of a Deccan sunset, and somewhere in the city beyond the walls — a city I could not see but whose sounds occasionally reached us — the ordinary life of ordinary people continued, as ordinary life always continues, indifferent to the particular dramas that prisons contain.

I had been in prison before. This was not my first encounter with British hospitality in this form, and I had learned, in previous imprisonments, that the mind can be an adequate companion if properly directed. One cannot simply wait for imprisonment to end. One must find, within the constrained space, an interior freedom that the walls cannot breach.

So I had determined to write — not in the way of journalism or politics, which are the modes of address to an immediate audience, but in the way of reflection, which addresses a longer time and a less defined readership.

India was the subject. But India is not a subject that admits of a beginning or an end. It has been a civilization for as long as anything has been a civilization. Its roots go so deep that to trace them requires moving through strata of time that the imagination can barely accommodate. And yet the effort seemed worth making — perhaps especially now, when the question of what India was and would be had taken on an urgency that only the proximity of independence could give it.

What is this India? What are the people of this vast subcontinent, so diverse in language and religion and custom, and yet — somehow, repeatedly — feeling themselves to be part of a single entity? What is the thread that runs through five thousand years of history, connecting the cities of the Indus to the courts of the Guptas to the philosophical debates of the Mughal period to the national movement of the present?

I did not expect to answer these questions. I expected, at best, to think about them carefully enough to understand why they resisted easy answer.

CHAPTER 2: The Quest
───────────────────────────────────────────

The quest for India had begun, in my own life, in an unexpected place: in the writings of the English Orientalists, those extraordinary British scholars of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries who had applied the methods of European philology and archaeology to the study of Sanskrit and Pali and Prakrit, and had produced, from their labors, an India that Indians themselves had largely forgotten.

This was one of the paradoxes of colonialism that I found endlessly interesting: the colonial power, in the course of administering what it had conquered, sometimes preserved and restored what the conquered had lost. The Orientalists were not doing this for the benefit of India; they were doing it for the benefit of European scholarship and, more practically, for the benefit of a British administration that needed to understand what it governed. But the work they did — the decipherment of Brahmi script, the translation of the Vedas into English, the archaeological surveys that began to reveal the extent of India's ancient urban civilization — became, ironically, a resource for Indian nationalism.

I had come to the Vedas and the Upanishads and the Mahabharata not in their original Sanskrit but in translation — first in English, then, as my own engagement deepened, in the original texts with the help of scholars who were generous with their knowledge. What I found was not what I had expected. I had expected — in the way of a modern, scientifically-minded young man — to find mythology: stories of gods and demons that might be interesting as literature but whose relationship to truth was metaphorical.

What I found instead was philosophy — and not primitive philosophy, but philosophy of considerable sophistication, engaging questions about the nature of consciousness and reality and the relationship between the individual and the universal that European philosophy had arrived at much later and, in some respects, had not surpassed.

This discovery was not simply intellectual. It was, in a way I would not have predicted, personal. I felt something click into place — the sense of a heritage that was mine and that I had not known was mine, and whose loss would have been a loss I would not have known to mourn.

CHAPTER 3: The Indus Valley Civilization
───────────────────────────────────────────

When the excavations at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa in the 1920s began to reveal the extent of the Indus Valley Civilization, they revealed something that changed the chronology of India's story in ways that are still being absorbed.

Here was a civilization — urban, sophisticated, evidently organized — that had flourished in the third and early second millennium BCE, contemporary with ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia, and in some respects their equal. The cities of the Indus valley had street grids, drainage systems, standardized weights and measures, and — most remarkably — a script that has not yet been deciphered, and may contain accounts of a world we cannot yet access.

What struck me about the Indus valley cities, reading the archaeological reports in prison, was not their antiquity alone — though the antiquity was astonishing — but their quality of life. The houses were substantial. The sanitation was, by the standards of the ancient world, advanced. The art — the small bronze figures, the carved seals, the terracotta toys — had a quality of delight and craft that suggested people who valued beauty and play alongside the more utilitarian necessities.

We do not know who these people were. We do not know what language they spoke, what gods they worshipped, what stories they told their children. The script that might tell us this has not yielded its secrets. And yet the civilization is undeniably Indian — not in the narrow ethnic or linguistic sense, but in the sense of belonging to the continuous story of this land, this river system, this particular encounter between human ingenuity and the subcontinent's diverse environments.

The Indus valley cities were abandoned — gradually or suddenly, we do not know which — sometime around 1700 BCE. What happened to their inhabitants? They did not disappear; populations do not disappear. They moved, and dispersed, and mixed with others who were moving through the subcontinent, and their descendants are the people of India today, carrying in their cultures — in their sanitation practices, their artistic traditions, their ways of organizing urban space — traces of what was built here five thousand years ago.

CHAPTER 4: The Vedic Period and Its Legacy
───────────────────────────────────────────

The Vedic hymns are among the oldest surviving literature of humanity, and they carry within them a quality that the oldest things often carry: the freshness of genuine encounter. These are not yet the hymns of an established religion, with its fixed doctrines and its professional interpreters. They are the hymns of people who were genuinely astonished by the world — by fire and water and wind and the mystery of the mind's own operation — and who expressed this astonishment in language of great beauty.

The Rigveda asks, in one of its most famous passages: "Who really knows? Who will here proclaim it? Whence was it produced? Whence is this creation? The gods came afterwards, with the creation of this universe. Who then knows whence it has arisen?" This is not a rhetorical question designed to assert a known answer. It is a real question, asked in genuine uncertainty, and the willingness to sit with that uncertainty — to ask the question without demanding the comfort of an immediate answer — seems to me one of the most admirable qualities of the Vedic mind.

The later Upanishads developed this inquiry in a more systematic direction — toward the concepts of Brahman and Atman, the universal consciousness and the individual self, and the relationship between them. The idea that the self (Atman) is in its deepest nature identical with the universal reality (Brahman) — "Aham Brahmasmi" (I am Brahman), "Tat tvam asi" (That thou art) — is a philosophical proposition of extraordinary audacity, proposing a continuity between the individual and the cosmos that collapses the usual hierarchies of significance.

Whether these propositions are literally true in some metaphysical sense is not a question I feel equipped to answer. But as a framework for approaching the world — as a set of attitudes toward the self and its relationship to other selves and to the larger reality — I find them generous and sustaining in a way that has made them useful throughout my life.

CHAPTER 5: The Buddha and His Message
───────────────────────────────────────────

The Buddha was born in the foothills of the Himalaya, in the Lumbini grove that is now in southern Nepal, sometime in the fifth or fourth century BCE. The exact date is disputed; what is not disputed is the quality of the revolution he initiated.

He was a prince of the Shakya clan — Siddhartha Gautama — who had been raised, according to the traditional account, in deliberate insulation from suffering. The story of his first encounter with old age, disease, death, and the wandering ascetic who had found a way to engage these realities, is told so often that it risks becoming merely legendary. But the philosophical problem that the story encodes is genuinely urgent: the problem of dukkha, of suffering in its broadest sense — not just pain, but the unsatisfactoriness of conditioned existence, the gap between what we want and what we have, the impermanence of everything we cling to.

The Buddha's response to this problem was, in its method, entirely empirical. He did not appeal to scripture or to divine revelation. He appealed to examination of experience — to the careful, sustained observation of the workings of the mind, and to the results of that observation: that suffering arises from craving, and that the cessation of suffering comes through the cessation of craving, achievable through ethical conduct and meditative discipline.

What strikes me most about the Buddha is not the metaphysics — though the metaphysics are profound — but the method. The insistence on examining rather than accepting. The refusal to demand belief in what has not been verified. The famous parable of the poisoned arrow: if a man is struck by a poisoned arrow, he does not need to know the caste of the archer, or the type of wood in the shaft, before he can pull out the arrow. The philosophical questions that cannot be answered need not prevent us from addressing the suffering that can be addressed.

CHAPTER 6: The Meaning of India
───────────────────────────────────────────

As I came toward the end of these months of writing — the sun moving through its arc above the battlements, the season shifting from the cool of winter to the first heat of spring — I found myself returning to the original question: what is this India?

It is not a nation in the European sense — not a state formed by the consolidation of a single language and culture and religion into a political unit. It is something older and stranger than that, and the attempt to make it conform to that European model has always done violence to what it actually is.

India is, rather, a civilization — a way of being in the world that has proven, over five thousand years, capable of absorbing enormous diversity and enormous disruption without losing the thread of its own particular consciousness. It has been invaded repeatedly — the Aryans, the Persians, the Greeks under Alexander, the Kushans, the Hunas, the Arabs, the Turks, the Mughals, the Europeans — and from each invasion it has absorbed what was useful and maintained what was essential.

This is not passivity. It is a kind of strength that the more rigid forms of identity cannot achieve — the strength of a river that can absorb many tributaries without ceasing to be itself.

The challenge of India's future — the India that is now, as I write this, approaching its independence — will be to understand what it actually is, rather than what ideology requires it to be. To acknowledge the diversity that is its actual condition, rather than imposing on it the uniformity that would make it manageable but would also make it dead. To build institutions adequate to its complexity, rather than institutions adequate only to its simplest possible description.

I believe this is possible. I believe it because I have spent these months tracing the thread of India's thought across five thousand years, and the thread has been there, continuous, however often it appeared to be broken. The capacity is there. The civilization that produced the Buddha and Ashoka and Akbar and Kabir and Ramanujan is capable of producing the institutions that justice requires.

Whether it will do so is not a matter of belief. It is a matter of will.
