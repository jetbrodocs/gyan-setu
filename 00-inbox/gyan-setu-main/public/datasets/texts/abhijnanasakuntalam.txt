          ABHIJNANASAKUNTALAM
          (The Recognition of Shakuntala)
          by Kalidasa
          ~4th–5th century CE (Gupta period)
═══════════════════════════════════════════

[Note: English translation/prose rendering for demo purposes.
Originally a Sanskrit nataka (play) in seven acts.
One of the most celebrated works of world literature.
Goethe wrote of it: "Wouldst thou the young year's blossoms and the fruits of its decline, and all by which the soul is charmed, enraptured, feasted, fed? Wouldst thou the earth and heaven itself in one sole name combine? I name thee, O Shakuntala! and all at once is said."]

───────────────────────────────────────────
ACT I: The Hunt and the First Sight
───────────────────────────────────────────

[The stage is a royal hunting ground, on the edge of a forest hermitage. A chariot is heard; voices.]

STAGE MANAGER'S BENEDICTION: May the Lord Shiva's eight forms — the five elements, the sun, the moon, and the sacrificing priest — protect and bless you all who have gathered here.

[A chariot enters. KING DUSHYANTA stands in pursuit of a deer.]

KING: The deer runs. My arrow follows. And yet — [he draws back] — what is this? A notice on the boundary post: "Animals of the hermitage are not to be harmed. This boundary is sacred ground."

[He lowers his bow. His charioteer draws up.]

CHARIOTEER: Maharaja, we have crossed into the hermitage of the sage Kanva.

KING: Then we have crossed into a different world, and we must behave accordingly. [He dismounts.] I will go on foot, and quietly, and with respect. The deer that led me here is safe.

[He enters the garden of the hermitage. Through the trees, he sees three young women tending plants. He is transfixed.]

KING: [to himself] Who are these women, moving with such grace through the forest garden? They are not born to the rough life of ascetics; there is about them a quality of cultivation, of elegance, that belongs to palaces rather than hermitages. Yet here they are, tending plants with hands that seem accustomed to delicate work. And that one — the one in the centre — who is she?

[SHAKUNTALA and her two companions, ANASUYA and PRIYAMVADA, tend the plants. They have not yet noticed the king.]

SHAKUNTALA: [touching the bark of a young tree] This mango tree is thriving. I have worried about it through the dry season, but look — new leaves already. Priyamvada, I think it has taken to this spot.

PRIYAMVADA: Of course it has. Everything thrives when you tend it.

ANASUYA: [noticing something] Shakuntala — I think we are observed.

[SHAKUNTALA looks up. For a moment, she and the king see each other. The text indicates that this is the moment — the encounter that both will carry forward into all that follows.]

KING: [to himself] She is the daughter of a king, I would wager my kingdom on it, despite the hermitage, despite the bark-cloth garment. There is that quality in her. And she looks at me with eyes that are — [he pauses, finding no adequate comparison] — she looks at me as the earth looks after the first rain.

───────────────────────────────────────────
ACT II: The Friend's Counsel
───────────────────────────────────────────

[A glade within the hermitage. The king's companion, the jester MADHAVYA, has followed at a distance. He is not comfortable in forests.]

MADHAVYA: Hunting in forests. Chasing deer. Sleeping on the ground. Your Majesty, I am the king's jester, not the king's hound. My feet have never done anything to deserve this treatment.

KING: [distracted] Madhavya, what do you know of the sage Kanva?

MADHAVYA: The sage Kanva? I know what everyone knows — that he is a great ascetic, that his hermitage is a place of considerable spiritual power, and that wise men do not make trouble there. Why?

KING: He has a ward. A young woman named Shakuntala.

MADHAVYA: [understanding immediately] Ah. The deer led you somewhere more interesting than a hunting ground.

KING: She is not an ordinary woman, Madhavya. She is — [he searches for the right word and abandons the search] — extraordinary. Her father, I have learned, was the royal sage Vishwamitra, before his renunciation. Her mother was the apsara Menaka. She was left here as a child, in Kanva's care.

MADHAVYA: The daughter of a sage and a celestial nymph, raised in a forest hermitage. And you are the king. This is a match that the poets write about.

KING: The poets would also write about the obstacles. She will not leave this place lightly. The hermitage is her world. And I cannot leave my duty.

MADHAVYA: What does she feel?

KING: [a long pause] I think she feels what I feel. I cannot be certain. But her eyes, when she looked — Madhavya, her eyes were not indifferent.

───────────────────────────────────────────
ACT III: The Declaration
───────────────────────────────────────────

[A bower of flowers within the hermitage. SHAKUNTALA sits alone, writing on a lotus leaf with a flower-petal stylus.]

SHAKUNTALA: [writing, softly] "I do not know his name. I do not know where he comes from. I know only that he looked at me from the edge of the garden, and that I have thought of nothing else since. This is not wisdom. This is something else."

[She hears a sound and hastily conceals the leaf.]

[The KING enters, having separated himself from his attendants.]

KING: She is here. [He approaches, then stops himself.] I came to say something definite, and now the words have arranged themselves in a different order than I intended. [Aloud] Forgive me — I have come into your bower uninvited. I sought shade.

SHAKUNTALA: [with composure] You are the guest of this hermitage. There is no uninvited guest here.

KING: I am Dushyanta. [A pause.] I have wanted to speak with you since the garden.

SHAKUNTALA: I know who you are.

KING: [surprised] How?

SHAKUNTALA: Anasuya made inquiries. She is thorough in these matters. [A brief, involuntary smile.]

KING: [encouraged by the smile] Then you know that I am a king, which means my time here is limited. The affairs of the kingdom do not pause for — [he searches] — for hermitage visits.

SHAKUNTALA: [quietly] I know that everything here is temporary. The flowers bloom and fall. The birds nest and leave. Even the sage Kanva goes away and returns. Nothing stays.

KING: Some things stay. If they are wanted to stay. [He pauses.] Shakuntala, I wish to speak plainly, because the time for indirection has passed. I have felt, from the moment I saw you, what I have only read about in poetry and dismissed as a poet's exaggeration. I know now that it is not exaggeration.

SHAKUNTALA: [her voice very quiet] I know too.

[They look at each other across the bower's fragrant air.]

───────────────────────────────────────────
ACT IV: The Curse of Durvasa
───────────────────────────────────────────

[This act turns on the intervention of fate. The sage Durvasa arrives at the hermitage while Shakuntala, her mind entirely occupied with thoughts of Dushyanta, fails to greet him with the hospitality protocol requires.]

DURVASA: [entering] I call out at the gate of this hermitage, and no one comes. I am Durvasa, and this discourtesy — [he sees Shakuntala, who has still not fully returned from wherever her mind has been] — this young woman, lost in some private world, ignoring the obligation of welcome —

[He speaks the curse before the anger that drives it can be tempered by the reason that follows.]

DURVASA: The one in whose thought you are so lost that you cannot see what is before your eyes — he shall forget you. As completely as you have forgotten your duty.

[He leaves. PRIYAMVADA runs in.]

PRIYAMVADA: Shakuntala! Did you not hear him? Durvasa was here — he called at the gate —

SHAKUNTALA: [coming fully present with horror] Durvasa? The sage whose curses —

PRIYAMVADA: He spoke a curse. I heard it.

[Both women are stricken. ANASUYA comes running.]

ANASUYA: I went after him. He is somewhat appeased — he says the curse will be broken when the king sees the ring he gave you. The token of recognition.

SHAKUNTALA: [touching the ring that Dushyanta placed on her finger as the seal of their union] This ring.

PRIYAMVADA: Yes. If he sees it, his memory will return.

[Shakuntala looks at the ring for a long moment.]

SHAKUNTALA: Then I must go to him. And I must not lose the ring.

───────────────────────────────────────────
ACT V: The Ring and the Recognition
───────────────────────────────────────────

[The royal court. DUSHYANTA sits on his throne. SHAKUNTALA enters, veiled, accompanied by a guardian sent from the hermitage. The king looks at her with no recognition.]

GUARDIAN: Your Majesty, the sage Kanva sends his ward Shakuntala to you. She carries — [he pauses with delicacy] — she carries proof of her connection to this court.

[SHAKUNTALA removes her veil. The king looks at her face with a frown of uncertainty — a face that seems somehow familiar and yet produces no specific memory.]

DUSHYANTA: I do not know this woman.

SHAKUNTALA: [the world ending quietly] Your Majesty — Dushyanta — I am Shakuntala. We were married at the hermitage of Kanva. You placed that ring upon my finger as your seal.

[She extends her hand. The ring is not there. It was lost — dropped in the river during her journey, taken by a fish. The single key to the curse has been lost.]

DUSHYANTA: [genuinely troubled, because something in him strains toward recognition that the curse blocks] I have no memory of what you describe. I am sorry.

SHAKUNTALA: [with great dignity, because she is her father's daughter and her mother's child, and dignity is what she has] I see. [A pause.] Then I will go.

[She leaves. The king watches her go with an expression that is not recognition but is not nothing, either. Something moves in him like a fish beneath dark water — not yet visible, but present.]

[Later — a fisherman brings a ring found in the stomach of a fish he has caught near the river road. The ring bears the royal seal. It is brought to the king. The moment it is in his hand, the curse breaks.]

DUSHYANTA: [with the abruptness of returned memory] Shakuntala.

───────────────────────────────────────────
EPILOGUE: The Reunion
───────────────────────────────────────────

The search took years. By the time Dushyanta found Shakuntala — in the celestial hermitage of the sage Marichi, in the divine realm between earth and heaven, where she had gone after her rejection — she had borne their son, a boy named Bharata who played fearlessly with lion cubs and counted their teeth.

The king arrived at the hermitage of Marichi and saw his son before he saw his wife. He saw this child — fearless, strong, entirely at home in a celestial garden — and felt the pull of recognition even before the identification was made. The child was his. He knew this without being told.

Then Shakuntala came out of the garden.

Kalidasa does not elaborate the reunion scene in the way of lesser writers, who would use it to make an emotional point. He shows it with restraint: two people who have been separated by fate, and by forgetting, and by time, seeing each other again with the full clarity that previous partial understanding could not achieve.

The curse is broken. The memory is returned. The child is named. And the play ends where all good plays end — not with the resolution of every difficulty, but with the sense that the essential has been restored: that the people who belong together are together, that the recognition has been accomplished.

Bharata, their son, will grow up to unite the nation that will bear his name — the great Bharatavarsha, the land of Bharata, the name by which India has known itself for millennia.

This too is in the play, carried in the son's name: the sense that from this particular love, this particular recognition, something much larger than a single story comes.
