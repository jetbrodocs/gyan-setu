PREFACE: Why I Write This
───────────────────────────────────────────

It is not my purpose to attempt a real autobiography. I simply want to tell the story of my numerous experiments with truth, and as my life consists of nothing but those experiments, it is true that the story will take the shape of an autobiography. But I shall not mind, if every page of it speaks only of my experiments. I believe, or at any rate flatter myself with the belief, that a connected account of all these experiments will not be without benefit to the reader. My experiments in the political field are now known, not only to India, but to a certain extent to the "civilised" world. For me, they have not much value; and the title of Mahatma that they have won for me has, therefore, even less. It is only through spiritual experiments that I have attained what little I know of truth.

The experiments I shall describe in this book are experiments in spiritual field which have no known place in my work either as a politician or as a person of wide repute. How, then, I am to write my story without writing much about my political and public career? That I cannot avoid if I am to tell the story of my experiments with truth. All these experiments I shall regard as spiritual matters; my political career is only a part of that. It may be that what I have done in the political field will be regarded as being unconnected with the story of my experiments with truth. But I think that is a mistake.

PART I, CHAPTER 1: Birth and Parentage
───────────────────────────────────────────

The Gandhis belong to the Bania caste and seem to have been originally grocers. But for three generations, from my grandfather, they have been Prime Ministers in several Kathiawad States. Uttamchand Gandhi, alias Ota Gandhi, my grandfather, must have been a man of principle. State intrigues compelled him to leave Porbandar, where he was Dewan, and to seek refuge in Junagadh. He saluted the Nawab of that State with the left hand. Someone, noticing the apparent discourtesy, asked for an explanation, which was given thus: "The right hand is already pledged to Porbandar."

Ota Gandhi contracted a second marriage after his first wife's death. He had four sons by his first wife and two by his second. I do not think that in my childhood I ever felt or knew that these sons of the second wife were not also the sons of the first. This was due to the extraordinary simplicity and humanity of my father's step-mother. She was a very religious woman.

My father, Karamchand Gandhi, alias Kaba Gandhi, who was the fourth son of Ota Gandhi, was for some years Prime Minister in Porbandar State and then in Rajkot and Vankaner. He was a member of the Rajkot State Council when he died. He was not educated, save that he had had the experience of practical affairs. At best, he could have been said to have had an education extending up to the fifth standard. He knew little of history or geography. But his rich experience of practical affairs stood him in good stead in the solution of the most intricate questions and in his management of hundreds of men. He never resorted to untruth in his relations, whether with his employers or those under him. His loyalty to the State was great. A thoughtful man, he was not given to much reading. He had, however, a good memory and had read considerably in Gujarati. Of his religion he was not sectarian. He had considerable respect for Vaishnavism, but was not a devotee of it. Being often in contact with Hindus and Mussalmans, he was also broad-minded. This left its impression on me.

PART I, CHAPTER 6: A Tragedy
───────────────────────────────────────────

Among the many evils that I have spoken of there was one that I must now speak of in some detail. I have said that there was a wave of reform amongst the educated young men of Rajkot at that time. Smoking cigarettes was quite the fashion amongst them. It was not that they smoked in their parents' presence, but they did so secretly. I also adopted this habit. I used to pick up the cigarette ends, which were left by my uncle, and smoke them. I grew so fond of it that I used to steal small coins to be able to buy cigarettes.

At the same time, I could not even bear to think of telling a lie. My brothers made the matter easy by borrowing money from their pocket and buying cigarettes. At this time I was in the lowest class of the secondary school. I had contracted the habit of smoking. It was not from any predilection for the thing. I wanted to smoke simply because I saw my elders smoking. I was the most junior of the three brothers I mentioned, and I feared that the elders might rebuke me. So I had recourse to theft.

The point I want to make is that in connection with this habit I did not even try to reason out why I should not smoke. I simply gave up the habit as suddenly as I had adopted it. I should not say that I had reflected upon the wrongness of the habit; I did not want to continue it, and I was afraid of discovery. This secret from my parents was a great burden. But the moment I had decided to give it up, I ceased to have the trouble of hiding it.

I made a vow never to smoke. And I have kept it to this day.

PART II, CHAPTER 1: Raychandbhai
───────────────────────────────────────────

In my search for a spiritual guide, the most important figure of my early life was Raychandbhai Mehta, also known as Srimad Rajchandra, a Gujarati poet and philosopher who was at that time engaged in the jewellery business in Bombay. He was a remarkable man — young, extraordinarily learned in the scriptures of multiple religious traditions, and possessed of a capacity for what he called samyak jnana, or right knowledge, that I had not encountered in anyone else.

What struck me about him first was not his words but his presence — the quality of attention he brought to a conversation, as though the subject of the conversation was the only thing in the world that existed at that moment. He was a busy man, surrounded by the demands of commerce, and yet when he spoke about dharma or the nature of the self, the commerce disappeared entirely.

He read voraciously in Sanskrit, Gujarati, and several other languages. He had committed vast portions of scripture to memory, not as a performance of piety but as a practical resource — he could be in the middle of a discussion and produce, without effort, an exactly relevant passage from the Jain canon or the Bhagavad Gita or the Upanishads, not to win an argument but to illuminate the question.

I wrote to him from South Africa with questions that were troubling me — about the nature of the soul, about the relationship between Hinduism and Christianity and Islam, about whether ahimsa was a practical principle or only an ideal. He answered each question with the seriousness it deserved, neither dismissing my doubts nor resolving them artificially. He guided me back to my own tradition without disparaging the others that had attracted my inquiry.

He died young, at thirty-three, having compressed into that short life more genuine thinking about religion than most men manage in eighty years. I have never found another spiritual guide who answered what he answered.

PART IV, CHAPTER 1: Advent into India
───────────────────────────────────────────

I returned to India from South Africa in 1915, after more than twenty years away, to a country I knew and did not know — knew from memory and childhood and the deep formation of language and landscape that is established before the age of reason; did not know in the specific contemporary sense that required knowing what the country was now, what its people were experiencing, what forms of life and struggle had developed in my absence.

Gokhale, my political guru, had advised me before my departure: "Go around India first. See it. Listen to it. Don't presume to lead it before you have learned it."

I took this advice seriously. I spent a year traveling — not in the first-class compartments that my Bombay friends assumed I would use, but in the third class, with the mass of the traveling Indian public. This was not an affectation. It was the education I required.

Third-class travel in India in 1915 was an education in the conditions of Indian life that no amount of reading or conversation could have provided. The overcrowding was not incidental but systemic — the infrastructure assumed that the majority of its users had needs that need not be accommodated beyond the minimum compatible with their arrival at a destination. The filth was the filth of a system that had not been designed by people who would use it, for people whose experience of using it was of no consequence to those who designed it.

But what I also found in third class was the energy and the texture of India itself — the extraordinary diversity of language and dress and food and custom, the capacity for human connection across difference that people in close quarters develop, the dignity that ordinary people carry under conditions that should make dignity impossible.

I met a family from the districts of Bengal. I met a widow traveling alone to a pilgrimage site, who conducted herself with a serenity that I found instructive. I met laborers going to the cities and farmers returning from markets and students on their way to examinations.

India was there, present, actual, in that third-class compartment. Not the India of speeches and memorials, but the India that existed in the daily life of three hundred million people — most of them working very hard, most of them in conditions of poverty that no civilization could be proud of, and most of them carrying within them a vitality and a spiritual depth that no poverty had been able to extinguish.

It was from this India that the work had to begin.
