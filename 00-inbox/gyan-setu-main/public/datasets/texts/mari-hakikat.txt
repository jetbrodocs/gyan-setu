          MARI HAKIKAT
          (My Truth / My Account)
          by Narmad (Narmadashankar Dave)
          Written 1866, Published posthumously 1933
═══════════════════════════════════════════

CHAPTER 1: The World Into Which I Was Born
───────────────────────────────────────────

I was born in Surat on the 24th of August, 1833, to a family of Nagar Brahmins who considered themselves respectable, which in those days meant they had a certain position in the social order, a certain capacity for ceremony, and a certain rigidity in both that they would not easily surrender.

My father, Dave Laxmishankar, was a man of moderate means and immoderate habit — he was fond of conversation, fond of gathering friends, and had the particular gift of being interesting to be around without ever being remarkable in the ways that produce wealth or stability. He loved me in the undisciplined way of fathers who love but do not know quite how to express it except through the indulgence of the child's enthusiasms.

My mother was the more serious figure in my early formation. She was a woman of the older school in the best sense: literate in the household arts, capable in management, and possessed of an internal life that she did not often speak of but that revealed itself in small, illuminating moments — a comment at the edge of a conversation, a choice that seemed simple but was clearly considered.

Surat in my childhood was a city at a particular hinge in its history. It had been the great port of the Mughals, the place where pilgrims to Mecca departed and where European traders had first established their factories two centuries before. By the time I was born, Bombay had eclipsed it commercially, and Surat was adjusting to its new position — still proud, still cultured, still possessed of a civic life that was in some ways richer for not being driven entirely by the urgency of commerce.

The streets of the old quarter where I grew up had the quality of palimpsest — layer upon layer of history visible in the texture of the buildings, the mixture of languages in the market, the coexistence of mosque and temple and the trading houses of Europeans, all weathered to a rough compatibility.

Into this world I was born, and from it I drew the first materials of myself.

CHAPTER 2: My Schooling and the Books That Made Me
───────────────────────────────────────────

My formal education began at the school run by the Mission, which in those days was where the more progressive Surat families sent their boys. The English they taught there was not the English of Shakespeare — that came later and came at my own initiative — but the English of administration and commerce, the language that the new rulers of the country had made indispensable for any young man who wished to participate in the institutions they had created.

I learned it willingly. Languages have never frightened me. They are instruments, and instruments are to be mastered. But I learned it without ever feeling that learning it required me to stop being what I was — a Gujarati, a man of this specific soil, with the specific stories and the specific aesthetic sensibilities that this soil produces.

The library at the Mission school was small but consequential. It was there that I first read Voltaire, in an English translation that was itself imperfect, and felt the particular excitement of an argument made with absolute clarity — the pleasure of a mind that refuses to pretend. It was there that I read the English Romantics, who taught me something about the relationship between a poet and his landscape that I then looked for, and found, in the Gujarati literature I had previously taken for granted.

But the books that formed me most deeply were not in the school library. They were in the collection of an older neighbor, a retired vakil named Tribhuvandas, who had gathered — over decades of curious acquisition — texts in Sanskrit, Persian, Braj Bhasha, and Gujarati that represented the breadth of the subcontinent's written life. He allowed me to borrow freely, and I borrowed freely, and in the space of my reading I began to understand that I lived at the intersection of multiple traditions, and that this intersection was not a confusion but an opportunity.

CHAPTER 3: The Fire of Reform
───────────────────────────────────────────

I must speak honestly about the reforming impulse, because this honesty is what this account owes to those who will read it.

I came to reform not through a single revelation but through an accumulation of observations that gradually made the existing order of things intolerable to me. I watched women in my own family living half-lives defined by custom rather than capacity. I watched the caste system produce the specific cruelties that systems produce when they have been in place long enough to seem natural. I watched my own community — educated, cultured, proud — perpetuate practices that education and culture and pride should have rendered impossible.

And I was angry. I should say this clearly, because there is a tendency in accounts of this kind to sanitize the anger into something more respectable — into "concern" or "dedication to principle." No. I was angry. The anger was the fuel.

I wrote. I gave talks at gatherings that were not always welcoming. I wrote in the Gujarati newspapers that were beginning to appear, in the new print culture that was creating new possibilities for public argument. I founded a society — the Buddhivardhak Sabha — dedicated to the propagation of rational thinking and the questioning of inherited assumptions.

The opposition was swift and in some cases vicious. I was accused of being anti-Hindu, of being corrupted by Western ideas, of destroying the fabric of society for the sake of vanity. These accusations were made by men who had no interest in the fabric of society as it affected anyone below their own station, and I found it difficult to take their concern for tradition seriously.

But I learned, painfully, that reform is not simply the application of correct ideas to incorrect practices. It is also a negotiation with the people you wish to change, and a negotiation requires a certain patience that anger does not always permit.

This is one of the things I wish I had understood earlier.

───────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER 4: Love, Marriage, and the Courage of Departure
───────────────────────────────────────────

I was married, in the manner of the time, before I was old enough to have formed any views about marriage. My first wife, Dhamkuvar, died young — a loss that I record here with the inadequacy that all such records have. The words available to describe grief are always smaller than the grief itself.

I married again, and in my second marriage I encountered something I had not expected: a companion. Narmadagauri was not the submissive figure that the social order prescribed for wives. She had opinions, which she expressed with a directness that I found, after my initial surprise, deeply satisfying. We argued. We agreed. We argued again. It was, in its way, the most honest relationship I had ever been in.

When I decided to move to Bombay to pursue my literary and reforming work more effectively, she supported this decision. This may seem unremarkable. It was not. The decision required her to leave a familiar social world, to accept a reduced material comfort, to trust that the work I was doing mattered enough to justify the cost.

Bombay in those years was extraordinary — a city that was inventing itself with a speed and a chaos that was exhilarating and exhausting in equal measure. The intellectual life of the city, in the Gujarati and Marathi communities, was reaching a kind of peak of energy: new newspapers, new theatres, new associations, new arguments. I threw myself into it with everything I had.

There were years of genuine achievement and years of grinding poverty and years that were both simultaneously. I wrote my best poetry during these years and some of my worst prose. I made enduring friendships and, through my temperament, which was not always easy, some enduring enmities.

But I was alive, in the sense that the word means when it means more than merely continuing.

───────────────────────────────────────────
CHAPTER 5: Writing as Life's Work
───────────────────────────────────────────

I want to speak of the Gujarati language, because it is the subject that underlies everything else in this account.

When I began writing seriously, Gujarati literature was in a peculiar condition: rich in certain forms — devotional poetry, business correspondence, folk forms — and impoverished in others. The language had not yet produced a secular poetry that could address the full range of human experience without the mediation of religious framework. It had not produced the kind of prose that would allow complex ideas to be explored and argued in the vernacular.

I decided — and I say this without false modesty, because modesty at this point would be a form of dishonesty — that producing this literature was among my responsibilities. Not mine alone; others would do it better in some respects. But the gap needed to be addressed, and I was there and I had the skills, and waiting for someone more qualified is the refuge of those who do not wish to begin.

I wrote "Narmakosh," the first modern Gujarati dictionary, because the language needed to see itself as a systematic entity, with a documented vocabulary, before it could claim full literary standing. I wrote poetry in forms that had not been used in Gujarati before, adapting meters from Sanskrit and from English to see what the language could carry. I wrote essays and polemics and the occasional fiction.

Not all of it was good. I know which of my works will survive and which will not, and I am at peace with this. What matters is not the survival of any particular text but the establishment of the possibility — the demonstration that the language could do what it needed to do, could carry thought and feeling and argument and beauty in forms that were its own.

I write these notes in 1866, in my middle years, uncertain how many years remain. The Jay Jay Garvi Gujarat that I wrote will outlast me; I am confident of this, not from vanity but from observation of how things persist in communities. The ideas about reform will find expression in other people's work and other people's lives.

The language will continue.

That is enough.
